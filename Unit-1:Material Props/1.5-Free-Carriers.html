Effective Mass of Electrons



Electrons in the conduction band of a semi conductor move within the crystal lattice, and they experience Coulumb's forces due to the bonds, the atoms, the other electrons, and the holes.
Since F = ma; the complicated forces that act upon the electron in a crystal lattice, can be embedded into an effective mass. With the effective mass, we can use Newton's laws to compute the motion of electrons in the crystal.

The effective of mass of an electron in a semiconductor can be less or greater that hte electron rest mass in vacuum. There are well developed techniques to compute and measure effective masses. In this course, we will describe electrons and holes in a crystal lattice by using Newton's laws with the appropriate effective mass.

For electrons in semiconductors, F = mn * a, where mn is the electron effective mass.

Effective Mass of Holes



The same thing occurs for holes in the valence band. Holes can be thought of as positively charged particles that respond to forces with an effective mass, mp*. In general, the hole effective mass will be different from the electron effective mass or it might be smaller depending on the particular properties of the semiconductor.

Energy and "crystal momentum"(electron in cb)





For electrons and holes in a crystal, we can plot energy vs momentum in a similar way. The energy is (m*v2)/2 where m * is the effective mass. For common semiconductors we can often describe enrgy vs momentum as a parabola, just as for a free particle, but the momentum or the crystal momentum is a little bit more complicated. crystal momentum = h-bar k.... h-bar is Planck's constant, h, divided by 2 pi. k is the wave vector, 2 pi / wavelength. Electrons behave both as particles and as waves. As waves, they have a wavelength or they have a wave vector. For a free electron wave, the momentum is h-bar times k, so we will usually plot energy vs k rather than energy vs momentum. For a classical particle, F = dp/dt while for an electron in a semiconductor F = d(h-bar k)dt

Conduction and Valence band

We have electrons in the conduction band while holes in the valence band. The energy versus momentum for electrons in the conduction band can be written as bottom of the conduction band, Ec plus its kinetic energy, p, squared over 2mn*. This is how energy vs. momentum for conduction band is described.

For the valence band, E(k) bends down in energy. It has to bend down with increasing momentum coz the states are all below the top of the valence band. Ev.
To understand what's going on here, think about a hot electron. The hot electron will be high in the conduction band but within a few femtoseconds, it will relax down to the lowest energy that it can relax to, which is the bottom of the conduction band.

What if we create a hot hole? A hot hole lies deep in the valence band, but the electrons in the valence band will drop down in energy and fill that hole. So the hole will move up and within a few femtoseconds the hole will be near the top of the valence band. After a few femtoseconds, all the electrons will be near the bottom of the conduction band and all of the holes are near the top of the valence band.



Direct gap semiconductors





The plots like above are called band structure plots. There are 2 different kinds of semiconductors.
There are semiconductors that emit light when electrons and holes recombine - these are called direct gap semiconductors. In a direct gap semiconductor, the minimum of the conduction band and the maximum of the valence band occur at the same crystal momentum, so when an electron and a hole recombine, it's an electron and a hole with the same momentum. These direct gap semiconductors like GaAs are useful as they emit light when carriers recombine.

Indirect gap semiconductors don't tend to emit light during the recombination processes. In their energy band diagram as above, the bottom of the conduction band and the top of the valence bamnd occur at differnt momentum. In these kinds of semiconductors, it is difficult for electrons and holes to recombine in a way that gives off light. Instead, recombination tends to produce heat rather than light.

An Aside: Band Structure vs. Band Energy Diagram



When we plot E(k), we are producing a band structure plot. A Band structure plot is a plot of energy versus crystal momentum - this quantity h-bark, where k= 2pi over wavelength, which behaves like the momentum of an electron in a crystal.

An energy band diagram is simply a plot of the bottom of the conduction band and the top of the valence band versus position. As the course progresses, the energy band diagrams will become more and more useful to us.
