Properties of common semiconductors


Semiconductors Group-IV Periodic Table




All the elements used to make semiconductors appear in Column IV of the Periodic Table or are a combination of elements in columns at equal distance of Column IV on each side. For example, two elemental semiconductor materials are silicon and germanium from Column IV. Another common compound is gallium arsenide (GaAs), Ga from Column III and As from Column V. The elements for zinc oxide (ZnO) are each two columns away from Column IV, Zn in Column II and O in Column VI. This, however, is not a coincidence. All of these chemical bonds yield an average of four valence electrons per atom. These valence electrons are shared between all the atoms in the silicon crystal.


Two key numbers


The first no. is bandgap. The separation btw the bottom of the conduction band and the top of the valence band. EG for Si is 1.12 electron Volts. The other key no. is the intrinsic carrier concentration which comes from breaking the covalent bonds and creating holes in the valence band and electrons in the conduction band. For Si, it's 1 x 1010 @ 300K.
Tight connection btw band gap & intrinsic carrier concentration. The latter depends exponentially on the former. As the band gap gets wider, it gets exponentially harder to break those bonds and the intrinsic carrier concentration gets exponentially smaller. As the bandgap gets smaller, it gets easier to create these electron-hole pairs and the semi-conductor has a larger & larger intrinsic carrier concentration.

Why is Si so common in electronics?



Inside the crystal, each atom has four nearest neighbours and all bonds are satisfied.
Surfaces are defects with many dangling bonds.
Silicon has a "native oxide (SiO2)" that ties up dangling bonds and passivates the surface.
SiO2 is a hard, high quality insulator that has the ability to tie up and passivate dangling bonds. The ability to passivate these defects renders them electrically inactive, so that we can build electronic devices on surfaces of silicon. Other semiconductors dont have the especially nice property, and this is what has led to the widespread use of silicon in so many different applications.. There are specific applications for which Si isnt suitable and 4 dat we have 2 find other semiconductors.

Recombination


Recombination is the process through which it's possible for an elctron in the conduction band to drop down in the valence band. After a recombination event, there is one fewer electron in the conduction band and one fewer hole in the valence band. Now there's some energy that has to go somewhere. One possibility is that the energy can go into light. photons can be emitted. This happens in some column III-IV seli conductors like GaAs called direct gap semiconductors used in light emitting diodes and lasers.
Another possibility is that the excess energy just heats up the lattice. This is what happens in silicon and other indirect gap semiconductors. The semiconductor gets hotter which usually degrades the performance of electronic devices.

Other properties

  • How fast do charge carriers move(mobility)?
  • What is the thermal conductivity?
  • At what electric field does the semiconductor "break down"?
  • Effective mass?

The faster the electrons move, the more current they can carry. The mobility and diffusion coeeficient are two important parameters for describing the electron and hole transport. Another parameter called effective mass is also important for electron and hole transport; we'll discuss effective mass later.

Frequently, it's also important to know what the thermal conductivity of the semiconductor is; coz we don't want our semiconductor devices to heat up so much. It's possible if we apply large voltages to semiconductors to have electric fields that are so strong that the field can rip electrons out of the covalent bonds. When that happens, the semiconductor breaks down. The breakdown field is an important parameter. There are several parameters that describe electron-hole recombination process.

S U M M A R Y

  • Silicon is the most commonly used semiconductor because it has a native oxide that passivates the surface.
  • Other semiconductors are used when Si isnt suitable (e.g. to make light-emitting devices). Si doesnt emit light efficiently when electrons and holes recombine
  • The bandgap and intrinsic carrier concentration are 2 key (related) parameters for a semiconductor.
